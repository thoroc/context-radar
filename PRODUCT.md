# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

Engineers and technical practitioners who run Claude Code or comparable coding agents (Codex, Cursor, Copilot, OpenCode)
and want to cut context-window token consumption. They arrive mid-task, weighing whether a given tool is worth adding to
their agent stack. They read the catalogue the way they read a spec sheet: scanning for the pick in a layer, checking
conflicts and licence, and confirming a tool is actively maintained before they trust it.

## Product Purpose

context-radar is a comparison catalogue of tools that reduce context-window token use in coding agents. Tools are
grouped by the layer they act on, rated with a verdict, and checked for conflicts, licence, and upstream activity.
Success is a practitioner leaving with a defensible decision (this tool, for this layer, with these caveats), not a
purchase. The ratings are decision support, not adoption advice.

## Positioning

The catalogue that rates context-reduction tools as layered, conflict-aware decision support, each verdict backed by
cited evidence with a confidence status, rather than a popularity list or a marketing roundup.

## Brand Personality

Technical, credible, and evidence-led. The voice is precise and restrained: it earns trust by showing its working (cited
sources, confidence statuses, honest "watch" and "drop" verdicts) rather than by selling. British English, no hype. It
should read like a well-kept engineering reference, not a launch page.

## Anti-references

Do not look like generic AI-tool marketing: no gradient text, no glassmorphism, no big-number hero-metric template, no
decorative gradients. Do not adopt SaaS landing-page cliches: no identical icon-card grids, no tracked-uppercase eyebrow
above every section, no 01/02/03 numbered scaffolding. Do not convey status or meaning with emoji where a consistent
token-driven component (the status dot, the verdict pill) should carry it. Avoid prize or marketing hyperbole; the
ratings are decision support, not promotion.

## Design Principles

Practise what you preach: a catalogue about reducing token waste should itself be lean, fast, and low-noise. Show the
working: every verdict is backed by cited evidence and a confidence status, so trust is earned rather than asserted.
Keep one vocabulary: the same status dot, verdict pill, and token palette everywhere, with meaning never riding on emoji
or a one-off colour. Support the decision, do not push it: rank and caveat, and let the reader own the choice. Treat
accessibility as table stakes: legible contrast, keyboard reach, and a responsive layout are not polish.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Body text sits at or above 4.5:1 contrast in both light and dark themes; meaning is never carried by
colour alone (status dots pair with text labels); the interface is fully keyboard reachable with a visible focus ring;
touch targets are at least 44px on small screens; and every animation has a prefers-reduced-motion fallback. Dark and
light themes are both first-class, with an explicit toggle.
